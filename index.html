About

I study the gap between how we assume people act and how they actually do.

My work sits at the intersection of economics, behavioral science, and strategy. I'm drawn to hard problems, the kind where the first model is wrong, the data is messy, and the answer actually matters. I care about understanding why outcomes happen, not just describing them.

I'm completing a dual degree at Columbia University and Sciences Po Paris in Economics and Political Philosophy, with a special concentration in Business Management at Columbia Business School. My training spans econometrics, experimental design, corporate finance, and strategic analysis across three countries and two intellectual traditions.

Currently: synthesizing climate macroeconomics for policymakers under Jeffrey Sachs, building cost-of-equity estimation pipelines from large-scale financial data, and teaching intermediate macroeconomics to 200 students at Columbia.

The throughline across all three is the same: rigorous analysis applied to decisions that have real consequences.

Econometrics · Columbia Senior Seminar · 2026

Climate Vulnerability and Sovereign Debt Accumulation

Panel cointegration study across 27 Small Island Developing States, 2000–2023. Finds that a one-unit increase in ND-GAIN vulnerability implies ~105 pp more sovereign debt in the long run — independently of income, fiscal policy, and global shock regimes. Identifies an asymmetric ratchet mechanism and provides empirical support for the UN Multidimensional Vulnerability Index.

Panel VECM Cointegration Climate Economics Sovereign Debt R
